## Citizens' Assembly on Lynetteholm - We Do Democracy.pdf

**Key Insight:** A citizens' assembly can restore democratic legitimacy to a contested decision not by reversing it, but by building the collective intelligence needed to implement it better.

**Tweet 1 (Conversation Starter):**
When a major city decision is already made but widely distrusted, what does democracy actually owe its citizens? A vote? An explanation? Or something harder — a genuine conversation about how to move forward together?

**Tweet 2 (Technical Insight):**
Copenhagen's Lynetteholm assembly used stratified random selection across age, gender, education, and municipality to produce 66 citizens who demographically mirrored the metro area. Not a focus group. Not activists. A miniature city — then gave it real policy input.

**Tweet 3 (Personal Insight):**
What strikes me about Lynetteholm: the assembly couldn't stop the project. That was the point. Removing the illusion of veto power made honest conversation possible. We often design participation around the wrong question — "should we?" when the real work is "how do we do this well?"

## Protocols, Not Platforms_ A Technological Approach to Free Speech _ Knight First Amendment Institute.pdf

**Key Insight:** The internet's drift from open protocols to centralized platforms didn't just change how we communicate — it quietly transferred enormous power over public discourse to a handful of private companies, and rebuilding on open protocols could redistribute that power back to users.

**Tweet 1 (Conversation Starter):**
What if the biggest threat to democratic speech isn't what platforms allow or ban — but the fact that a handful of private companies get to make that call at all? Who should decide the rules of our digital public square?

**Tweet 2 (Technical Insight):**
Open protocols like SMTP or HTTP let anyone build a compatible interface — no single owner, no single moderator. If social discourse ran on open protocols instead of platforms, content filtering would be a user choice, not a corporate one. Competition replaces censorship debates.

**Tweet 3 (Personal Insight):**
Building tools for democracy, I keep hitting the same wall: we're trying to fix speech problems at the moderation layer when the real problem is the architecture layer. Centralizing power was a business decision, not an inevitable one. We can design differently.
